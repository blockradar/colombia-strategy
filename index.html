Thesis

In Colombia, the user holds USDC for months, not seconds. The balance is the product. The transaction is a side effect.

Whoever provides infrastructure that lets fintechs own the daily balance — not just route transactions — captures the next decade of LATAM cross-border. Blockradar's job is to be that infrastructure layer for Colombian fintechs.

The competitive moat isn't owning every pillar — it's being the wallet-infra layer that lets a Colombian fintech assemble the full closed loop without integrating five different vendors. Where We Compete on the Stack

Four player types are converging on the same closed loop · only one approach has all four pieces
Banks have trust but no stablecoin rails. Crypto exchanges have liquidity but compete with their own customers. Neobanks have UX but no cross-border crypto liquidity. Wallet-as-a-service infrastructure is the only seat without a structural conflict — and once rails and yield ship, it's also the most complete. Today we're at parity on shipped pieces (2/4) but no other player can credibly close the gap on rails + yield without abandoning their core business model. That's the seat we take.

Reading the Flow

Five stages, left to right. Money enters as Colombian pesos, gets tokenized, passes through our infrastructure, and exits as a real-world payment.

01
Fiat & Rails
COP enters via Bre-B (now the giant — $46B+/yr in just 6 months of operation), CO Banks (massive intra/inter-bank flows), PSE ($13B/yr in e-commerce), Efecty (cash network, last-mile), or Cobre (API rails, ~$1B TPV). Bre-B has eaten the rail layer — Cobre is still our best partner because it's API-first and Bre-B-native, but the underlying volume runs through the central bank now.
→
02
Stablecoins
USDT ($186B mkt cap) and USDC ($75B mkt cap) dominate — and we already support both, no partnership needed. The COP-pegged stablecoins (COPM ~$25M, COPW ~$15M, nCOP ~$3M) are orders of magnitude smaller but strategically matter because they're the only way to keep value in COP on-chain. The Minteo and Wenia partnerships are about positioning for COP-stablecoin growth, not chasing volume that exists today.
→
03
Wallet Infrastructure
This is the market we're entering. Blockradar isn't on the diagram — we have ~0 CO clients today. The competitive field: Fireblocks (~$2B/yr) dominates enterprise. In-house systems (~$1.5B/yr) handle the big neobanks. Bitso Business (~$120M/yr) is the LATAM B2B incumbent for fintechs. VelaFi (~$30M/yr) and Mural Pay (~$25M/yr) sit at our scale-target. Our shot is convincing the embedded-API layer to pick us over Bitso Business on developer experience and infra-only positioning (we don't compete with our customers).
→
04
Distribution
Five customer channels: embedded finance APIs (Mono, Kapital, Akua, Yuno) is now the biggest stripe — these companies need stablecoin modules but won't build them in-house. Crypto-native wallets (Littio, Qash, Meru) and MOVii on the mass-market side. Mass-market giants (Nequi, DaviPlata) are flagged but parked at Monitor due to capacity mismatch.
→
05
End-Use
Remittances and dollar savings are the largest end-use stripes — these are the use cases driving stablecoin demand in Colombia. Merchant settlement and FX/treasury are smaller but defensible niches that Mono/Kapital-style embedded clients want to serve.
★ Reading This Honestly
The diagram shows the market we're entering, not the market we're in. Fireblocks, in-house DIY, Bitso Business, VelaFi, and Mural Pay own the wallet-infrastructure layer in Colombia today. Blockradar has ~0 CO clients, which is why we don't appear at all. Our wedge is the embedded-finance APIs (Mono, Kapital, Akua, Yuno) and crypto-native wallets (Littio, Qash, Meru) where Bitso Business is the default but not locked in. Mass-market neobanks mostly use in-house custody (Wenia for Bancolombia, Nucripto for Nubank), so that's a future play. The real competitive battle is winning the embedded-API layer over Bitso Business on developer experience and infra-only positioning — and earning a node on this diagram in the next 12 months.
